## Key Concepts Learned

### Alert Triage

Alert triage is the process of reviewing and prioritizing security alerts.

A SOC analyst must determine whether an alert is benign, suspicious, or malicious. The analyst must also decide whether the alert should be closed, investigated further, or escalated.

Important triage questions include:

- What triggered the alert?
- Which user or system is involved?
- What time did the activity happen?
- Is the activity expected or unusual?
- Is there evidence of compromise?
- Should this be escalated?

### Alert Reporting

SOC analysts need to document their findings clearly. Good reporting helps other analysts, managers, and incident response teams understand what happened and what actions were taken.

A good report should include:

- Summary of the alert
- Affected users or systems
- Indicators of compromise
- Evidence reviewed
- Timeline of activity
- Analyst decision
- Recommended next steps

### Workbooks and Lookups

Workbooks and lookups help SOC analysts organize information and speed up investigations.

They can include:

- Known malicious IP addresses
- Suspicious domains
- User information
- Asset information
- Previous incidents
- Investigation checklists
- Escalation procedures

### SOC Metrics and Objectives

SOC teams use metrics to measure performance and improve security operations.

Common SOC metrics include:

- Mean Time to Detect
- Mean Time to Respond
- Number of alerts handled
- False positive rate
- Escalation rate
- Incident closure time
- Alert severity distribution

### Introduction to Phishing

Phishing is one of the most common attack methods against organizations.

Phishing investigations may include reviewing:

- Sender address
- Email headers
- Links
- Attachments
- Domain reputation
- User reports
- Suspicious language
- Login pages
- Indicators of compromise
